# Can I Eat Shrimp During Pregnancy? Safety Guide

*Written by the ProHealthIt Editorial Team | Sources cited below*

---

## Quick Answer

✅ **SAFE** — Shrimp is an excellent protein source during pregnancy with minimal mercury risk (0.009 ppm average). Cook to an internal temperature of 145°F (63°C), and consume as part of the FDA's recommended 2-3 weekly servings of low-mercury seafood.

---

## The Short Answer

Yes, shrimp is completely safe and healthy during pregnancy. This is one of the few foods where the scientific evidence is unequivocal—shrimp ranks among the lowest-mercury seafood available, making it one of the safest protein sources you can choose. A 3-ounce serving contains only 0.009 ppm of mercury, and ACOG and FDA both actively recommend seafood like shrimp for pregnancy nutrition. Shrimp provides exceptional protein density, selenium for thyroid and immune health, and astaxanthin, a powerful antioxidant. Like all seafood, cook it to 145°F (63°C) internal temperature to eliminate bacterial concerns. You can safely include shrimp in your diet 2-3 times per week as part of the FDA's recommended low-mercury seafood pattern, enjoying it in traditional preparations, stir-fries, or even cooked sushi rolls.

## Why Shrimp Matters During Pregnancy

Shrimp provides a concentrated package of pregnancy-supporting nutrients in a low-calorie, high-protein format. Its unique nutrient profile and minimal environmental contaminant load make it an ideal protein source during gestation.

**Exceptional protein-to-calorie ratio**: Shrimp delivers 17-20 grams of complete protein per 3-ounce serving with only 85-90 calories, making it one of the most protein-efficient foods available. During pregnancy, protein requirements increase by approximately 25 grams daily (from 46 grams to 71 grams daily), primarily to support fetal tissue development, placental growth, and expanded maternal blood volume. Shrimp's high protein content relative to caloric density makes it particularly valuable during pregnancy when weight gain must be carefully balanced with nutritional needs.

**Selenium and immune function**: Shrimp provides 36 mcg of selenium per 3-ounce serving—approximately 65% of the daily requirement. Selenium is essential for thyroid hormone metabolism, antioxidant defense through glutathione peroxidase, and immune system development. Adequate selenium during pregnancy supports both maternal immune function and fetal immune system programming.

**Astaxanthin and antioxidant protection**: The pink-red coloring of shrimp comes from astaxanthin, a carotenoid with potent antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. Astaxanthin penetrates cell membranes effectively, providing protection against oxidative stress in both maternal tissues and the developing fetus. Research suggests astaxanthin may support cardiovascular health and reduce inflammation—both important during pregnancy.

**Choline for fetal brain development**: Shrimp contains approximately 56 mg of choline per 3-ounce serving, contributing to the 450 mg daily recommendation during pregnancy. Choline is essential for fetal brain development, neural tube closure, and lifelong cognitive function. Adequate maternal choline intake correlates with improved fetal brain development and enhanced childhood cognitive outcomes.

**Mercury safety**: Unlike larger predatory fish that accumulate mercury through biomagnification, shrimp are crustaceans (not fish) that occupy a lower trophic level in marine food chains. Shrimp accumulate minimal mercury, with average mercury content of only 0.009 ppm—exceptionally low compared to recommended limits. This makes shrimp one of the safest protein sources available during pregnancy, free from the mercury concerns that justifiably restrict consumption of larger fish species.

## How to Safely Eat Shrimp During Pregnancy

Shrimp is inherently safe when properly handled and cooked. The primary food safety consideration involves cooking temperature and avoiding cross-contamination during preparation.

**Cooking temperature guidelines**:
Guidelines suggest cooking shrimp to 145°F (63°C) internal temperature, which eliminates pathogenic bacteria including Listeria, Salmonella, and Vibrio species that can contaminate raw shellfish. Cooked shrimp turns opaque white (rather than translucent/gray) when properly cooked; this visual indicator provides a reliable guide even without a thermometer. Shrimp cooks very quickly—typically 2-3 minutes per side in a pan or 3-4 minutes when boiled.

**Cooking methods and techniques**:
- **Pan-frying**: Heat oil in a skillet over medium-high heat; cook shrimp 2-3 minutes per side until opaque
- **Boiling**: Bring water to a boil, add shrimp, and simmer for 3-4 minutes until color changes to opaque
- **Grilling**: Place on skewers or in a grill basket; cook 2-3 minutes per side
- **Baking**: Spread on baking sheet, brush with oil, bake at 400°F (205°C) for 8-10 minutes
- **Steaming**: Steam for 4-5 minutes in a steamer basket over boiling water
- **Sautéing**: Cook briefly in a hot pan with vegetables and sauce for 3-4 minutes total

All methods reliably achieve the required 145°F (63°C) temperature.

**Shrimp selection guidance**:
Both wild-caught and farmed shrimp are safe during pregnancy. Farmed shrimp is more affordable and consistently available, while wild-caught shrimp may have slightly lower environmental impact depending on fishing methods. Fresh shrimp typically has a mild ocean smell; strong ammonia or "off" smells indicate bacterial overgrowth and are best avoided. Frozen shrimp (flash-frozen at harvest) is nutritionally equivalent to fresh shrimp and often fresher than "fresh" shrimp that has been transported and stored for days.

**Storage and handling**:
ACOG recommends storing raw shrimp in the coldest section of the refrigerator at 32-40°F (0-4°C) and using it within 1-2 days. Frozen shrimp maintains quality for 3-4 months in the freezer. Thaw frozen shrimp in the refrigerator overnight rather than at room temperature. Use separate cutting boards for raw shrimp and other foods, and wash hands, utensils, and surfaces after handling raw seafood to prevent cross-contamination with other foods.

**Frequency guidelines**:
The FDA recommends pregnant individuals consume 2-3 servings (8-12 ounces total) of low-mercury fish and shellfish per week. Shrimp is classified as a low-mercury shellfish, making it appropriate for this full recommendation. Pregnant individuals can safely consume shrimp multiple times per week without approaching any mercury or contaminant limits.

## Trimester-Specific Considerations

**First Trimester (Weeks 1-13):**
Early pregnancy establishes foundational development across all organ systems. Protein from shrimp supports amniotic fluid production and placental development. If morning sickness makes shrimp preparation challenging, brief cooking methods (pan-frying or boiling) may produce less odor than longer cooking. Some pregnant individuals find cold shrimp salads more appealing than hot preparations.

**Second Trimester (Weeks 14-27):**
Fetal growth accelerates, dramatically increasing protein requirements. Energy and appetite typically normalize during this trimester, making regular shrimp consumption feasible. Guidelines suggest incorporating shrimp into meal rotations 2-3 times weekly to optimize protein intake. Selenium intake becomes increasingly important for supporting expanded immune function and antioxidant needs.

**Third Trimester (Weeks 28-40):**
Continued protein intake supports rapid fetal weight gain (approximately 50% of birth weight is gained in the final trimester) and placental nutrient transfer. Shrimp consumption remains valuable through delivery. Some pregnant individuals experience decreased appetite or heartburn in late pregnancy; small shrimp portions incorporated into soups or light preparations may be more tolerable than larger portions.

## FAQ

**Q: Isn't shellfish mercury contamination a concern during pregnancy?**

A: Shrimp has exceptionally low mercury content (0.009 ppm average)—among the lowest of all protein sources available. The FDA specifically identifies shrimp as a "best choice" low-mercury seafood appropriate for pregnant individuals and young children. Mercury concerns primarily apply to larger predatory fish, not shrimp (FDA mercury database, 2024).

**Q: Can I eat raw shrimp or shrimp ceviche during pregnancy?**

A: No. Raw shrimp risks contamination from Vibrio bacteria and parasites that can cause severe illness during pregnancy. Shrimp must be cooked to 145°F (63°C) internal temperature. However, cooked shrimp in sushi rolls (such as shrimp tempura or cooked shrimp nigiri) is entirely safe.

**Q: Are farmed shrimp safe compared to wild-caught?**

A: Both farmed and wild-caught shrimp are safe during pregnancy with equivalent mercury levels and nutritional profiles. Farmed shrimp is more affordable and consistently available. Environmental sustainability concerns exist with some shrimp farming practices, but food safety and nutritional value are equivalent between farming methods.

**Q: What if I'm allergic to other shellfish—should I avoid shrimp?**

A: Shellfish allergies typically fall into two categories: crustacean allergy (shrimp, crab, lobster) and mollusk allergy (clams, mussels, oysters). Individuals allergic to crustaceans should avoid shrimp. However, some individuals tolerate crustaceans while reacting to mollusks, or vice versa. If you have a shellfish allergy, consult with your healthcare provider about which specific shellfish types are safe for your pregnancy.

**Q: Is there a limit to how much shrimp I can eat?**

A: No mercury-based limit exists for shrimp, making it one of the few seafood sources without consumption restrictions. You can include shrimp within your 2-3 weekly servings of low-mercury fish and shellfish. However, sodium content in shrimp is higher than some fish (190-210 mg per 3-ounce serving); if managing sodium intake for preeclampsia prevention, monitor total sodium across all foods.
