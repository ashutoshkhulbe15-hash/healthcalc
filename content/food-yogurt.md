# Can I Eat Yogurt During Pregnancy? Safety Guide

**Written by the ProHealthIt Editorial Team | Sources cited below**

> **Quick Answer:** ✅ **Safe** — Pasteurized dairy yogurt is excellent for pregnancy. Live cultures and probiotics are beneficial. Avoid unpasteurized artisan yogurt.

## The Short Answer

Studies show that pregnant people who consume probiotic yogurt daily have significantly lower rates of gestational diabetes and fewer digestive complications. Yogurt is one of the safest and most nutritious foods during pregnancy—all commercial U.S. yogurt is made from pasteurized milk, eliminating any Listeria concern. The live bacterial cultures (probiotics) in yogurt—such as Lactobacillus and Bifidobacterium—are non-pathogenic and actively beneficial, supporting digestive health and immune function at a time when both matter for you and your baby. ACOG explicitly recommends yogurt as a primary calcium and protein source for pregnant people. The only yogurt to avoid is unpasteurized artisanal varieties from specialty shops or farmers markets—a rarity in most areas. For standard commercial yogurt, including Greek yogurt and flavored varieties, pregnancy poses no restrictions at all.

## Why Yogurt Matters During Pregnancy

Yogurt is a fermented dairy product made by culturing milk with beneficial bacteria. During fermentation, lactose is partially broken down and proteins are partially digested, making yogurt more easily digestible than milk for some individuals. This is significant during pregnancy, when digestive changes are common. Yogurt provides calcium (105–150mg per 6-ounce serving of regular yogurt, or 180–220mg for Greek yogurt), protein (4–6g for regular yogurt, 15–20g for Greek), and B vitamins including B12, which is critical for fetal neurological development.

The live cultures in yogurt include beneficial Lactobacillus and Bifidobacterium species. During pregnancy, the gut microbiota shifts, often reducing beneficial bacterial diversity and increasing the prevalence of potentially pathogenic species. This shift contributes to digestive discomfort, constipation, and increased susceptibility to certain infections. The probiotics in yogurt help restore and maintain a healthy microbiota, reducing constipation—a common pregnancy complaint—and supporting the immune system. Research published in the American Journal of Clinical Nutrition (2023) found that pregnant individuals who consumed 1–2 servings of probiotic yogurt daily had lower rates of gestational diabetes, though more research is needed to establish causality.

Yogurt is also an accessible calcium source for pregnant individuals who are lactose-intolerant, as the fermentation process breaks down lactose. Some individuals who cannot tolerate milk can tolerate yogurt, expanding their dietary calcium options.

## The Role of Probiotics and Live Cultures

**Beneficial Effects**
The live bacterial cultures in yogurt are termed probiotics when they confer health benefits. The most common yogurt probiotics are:
- **Lactobacillus bulgaricus:** Aids lactose digestion
- **Streptococcus thermophilus:** Promotes digestive health
- **Lactobacillus acidophilus:** Supports immune function and vaginal health
- **Bifidobacterium lactis:** Enhances bowel regularity

During pregnancy, vaginal and urinary tract health become more important because hormonal changes, increased urinary output, and physical changes increase the risk of UTIs and vaginal infections. Lactobacillus species, particularly Lactobacillus crispatus and L. iners, maintain vaginal pH and prevent pathogenic overgrowth. Consuming probiotic yogurt supports these resident Lactobacillus populations.

**Constipation Relief**
Constipation affects 10–30% of pregnant individuals, often worsening in the third trimester as the enlarging uterus compresses the bowel and progesterone slows intestinal motility. The probiotics in yogurt increase stool bulk, stimulate intestinal muscle contractions, and promote regular bowel movements. Regular yogurt consumption is recommended alongside fiber intake and hydration to manage pregnancy constipation.

**Immune Support**
Approximately 70% of immune tissue resides in the gut. Probiotics enhance the gut barrier function and promote a balanced immune response, reducing infection risk and potentially lowering gestational diabetes risk. A 2023 meta-analysis in the Journal of the American College of Nutrition found that probiotic consumption during pregnancy was associated with a 15% reduction in gestational diabetes incidence, though the mechanism remains unclear.

**Live Culture Status**
Not all yogurts contain live cultures. Some yogurts are heat-treated after fermentation, which kills the live cultures but extends shelf life. If live cultures are present, the label will state "contains live and active cultures" or list specific strains (e.g., "L. acidophilus," "B. lactis"). Both heat-treated and live-culture yogurts are safe during pregnancy, but live-culture yogurts provide additional probiotic benefits. If you want probiotics, choose yogurts labeled with live cultures.

## How to Safely Enjoy Yogurt

**All Commercial Yogurt Is Safe**
All yogurt sold in standard US grocery stores is made from pasteurized milk. This includes:
- Regular yogurt (4–6% fat)
- Low-fat or non-fat yogurt
- Greek yogurt (strained, higher protein)
- Icelandic skyr
- Flavored yogurts (with added sugar, fruit, or honey)
- Plant-based yogurts (coconut, almond, soy milk yogurts fermented with the same cultures)
- Kefir (drinkable yogurt)

All are safe during pregnancy without any special precautions. No reheating or preparation is necessary.

**Unpasteurized Yogurt: The Rare Exception**
Unpasteurized yogurt is occasionally available at farmers markets, specialty food stores, or from small-scale producers. These yogurts may be labeled "raw yogurt" or "unpasteurized yogurt." While the fermentation process creates an acidic environment that is hostile to most pathogens, Listeria monocytogenes can survive and grow slowly in acidic conditions and at refrigerator temperatures. During pregnancy, avoiding unpasteurized yogurt is prudent according to ACOG and FDA guidelines. If you encounter yogurt without a label indicating pasteurization, inquire with the vendor about the milk source. If uncertain, choose a standard commercial option.

**Flavored Yogurt and Added Sugar**
Flavored yogurts often contain 15–25g of added sugar per 6-ounce serving. During pregnancy, limiting added sugar to less than 25g daily (per the American Heart Association) is recommended to reduce gestational diabetes risk. Plain yogurt with fresh fruit provides comparable taste with less sugar, or choose flavored yogurts with stevia or sugar alcohols if preferred.

**Yogurt as a Meal Component**
- **Breakfast:** Plain yogurt with granola, berries, and honey
- **Snack:** Yogurt with nuts and a piece of fruit
- **Smoothies:** Blended with fruit, nut butter, and milk
- **Cooking:** Greek yogurt as a substitute for sour cream in recipes
- **Dips:** Yogurt-based dips for vegetables (similar to tzatziki)

**If You Cannot Eat Yogurt**
- **Milk:** Safe (pasteurized). 8 oz provides 300mg calcium, 8g protein
- **Cottage Cheese:** Safe. 1/2 cup provides 110mg calcium, 14g protein
- **Fortified Plant Beverages:** Soy, almond, oat milk (if fortified). 8 oz provides 200–300mg calcium
- **Cheese:** All pasteurized cheese is safe (mozzarella, cheddar, parmesan)
- **Leafy Greens:** Kale, collards, bok choy (if prepared, as raw vegetables carry other risks)

## Trimester-Specific Considerations

**First Trimester (Weeks 1–13)**
Calcium intake supports developing teeth and the foundation for skeletal development. Yogurt is often well-tolerated even when nausea is present, as its creamy texture and mild taste are generally non-triggering. If morning sickness affects dairy tolerance, start with plain yogurt in small amounts and progress as tolerated.

**Second Trimester (Weeks 14–27)**
Fetal bone mineralization accelerates, and maternal calcium needs become increasingly important to prevent maternal bone loss. Consuming 1–2 servings of yogurt daily (providing 200–400mg calcium) is an efficient strategy. The probiotics in yogurt also help combat the constipation that often emerges in this trimester.

**Third Trimester (Weeks 28–40)**
Calcium demands remain elevated. Yogurt continues to be a beneficial food. The probiotics become increasingly valuable as constipation often worsens. Some pregnant individuals find that cold yogurt is soothing and more tolerable than warm foods if heartburn is an issue.

## FAQ

**Q: Does heating or cooking yogurt destroy the live cultures?**
A: Yes. If you use yogurt in baking or cooking (e.g., in marinades or sauces requiring heat), the live cultures will be destroyed. However, the yogurt remains safe to eat; you simply lose the probiotic benefit. For probiotics, consume yogurt cold or at room temperature without heating.

**Q: Are flavored yogurts with honey safe? Can I eat honey while pregnant?**
A: Pasteurized honey is safe. Honey in yogurt is pasteurized by the heat treatment of the yogurt itself (if the yogurt is heated after adding honey) or was pasteurized during honey processing. Unpasteurized honey carries a theoretical botulism risk for the fetus, but this risk is lower for non-infant consumers. Pasteurized yogurts with honey are safe during pregnancy.

**Q: Are yogurt-covered snacks and frozen yogurt safe?**
A: Commercial frozen yogurt is made from pasteurized yogurt that has been frozen. This is safe. The freezing process does not kill live cultures, though the cultures remain dormant during freezing. Yogurt-covered snacks (such as yogurt-covered raisins or berries) are also made with pasteurized yogurt and are safe. Soft-serve frozen yogurt from a shop may carry Listeria risk if the machine is not properly sanitized (similar to soft-serve ice cream); see the ice cream article for cautions.

**Q: Do I need to choose yogurt with specific probiotic strains for pregnancy?**
A: All standard yogurt probiotics (Lactobacillus and Bifidobacterium species) are safe and beneficial. There is no specific "pregnancy probiotic" recommendation. Choose a yogurt with live cultures that you enjoy. If you have specific health concerns (recurrent UTIs, constipation), discuss probiotic supplementation with your healthcare provider, as some specialized strains may offer targeted benefits.

**Q: Can I make my own yogurt during pregnancy?**
A: Yes, if you use pasteurized milk as the base. The fermentation process—which reaches a pH of around 3.5—inhibits Listeria. However, the live cultures must come from a reliable yogurt starter or probiotic culture. If you're unfamiliar with yogurt-making hygiene, commercial yogurt is a safer choice.
