# Can I Eat Salmon During Pregnancy? Safety Guide

*Written by the ProHealthIt Editorial Team | Sources cited below*

---

## Quick Answer

✅ **SAFE** — Salmon is one of the healthiest seafood choices during pregnancy. Cook to an internal temperature of 145°F (63°C), and consume 2-3 servings per week as recommended by the FDA. Salmon is an excellent source of protein, omega-3 DHA, and vitamin D.

## The Short Answer

> **Key Takeaway:** Salmon is one of the BEST foods to eat during pregnancy. It's high in omega-3 DHA (critical for baby's brain development), low in mercury, and rich in protein. Cook to 145°F internal temperature.

Studies show that pregnant individuals who regularly consume fatty fish like salmon have children with higher cognitive scores and better neurodevelopment outcomes. This isn't coincidence—it's the power of DHA, the omega-3 fatty acid that salmon delivers in exceptionally high amounts.

Salmon is an excellent food choice during pregnancy and actively recommended by ACOG and FDA guidelines. Both wild-caught and farmed salmon are safe when cooked to 145°F (63°C) internal temperature, and salmon's low mercury content makes it one of the safest seafood options available.

The FDA recommends 2-3 servings (8-12 ounces) of low-mercury fish weekly during pregnancy, and salmon exceeds these recommendations nutritionally. A single serving of salmon provides enough DHA to significantly support fetal brain development, eye health, and long-term neurodevelopment—benefits that extend well beyond birth.

## Why Salmon Matters

> **Note:** The FDA and ACOG specifically recommend 2-3 servings of low-mercury fish per week during pregnancy. Salmon is at the top of their recommended list — it's not just safe, it's actively beneficial. During Pregnancy

Salmon is among the most nutrient-dense foods available and provides multiple essential nutrients that support optimal fetal development. Its combination of high-quality protein, omega-3 fatty acids, vitamins, and minerals makes it uniquely valuable during pregnancy.

**DHA and fetal brain development**: Docosahexaenoic acid (DHA) is an omega-3 polyunsaturated fatty acid concentrated in the fetal brain and retina. During pregnancy, approximately 50-60 grams of DHA accumulates in fetal tissues, particularly in the third trimester when brain development accelerates dramatically.

Research demonstrates that maternal DHA intake correlates with improved fetal neurodevelopment, higher birth weight, and enhanced cognitive function in infancy (Oken E et al., Am J Obstet Gynecol 2005).

DHA cannot be synthesized by the human body and must be obtained through diet; salmon is one of the richest naturally occurring sources of DHA, containing approximately 1,480 mg per 3-ounce serving.

**Mercury concerns and safety**: Many pregnant individuals avoid fish due to mercury concerns, yet salmon presents minimal mercury risk. Mercury accumulation is directly proportional to fish size and lifespan; larger, longer-lived predatory fish (such as shark, swordfish, and king mackerel) accumulate mercury through biomagnification.

Salmon, which are smaller and shorter-lived than these species, accumulate significantly less mercury. The average mercury content in salmon is approximately 0.05-0.08 ppm (parts per million), well below the FDA's action level of 1.0 ppm and among the lowest of all seafood species.

**Complete protein source**: Salmon provides all nine essential amino acids in a single serving, making it a complete protein source. A 3-ounce serving of salmon contains approximately 17-20 grams of protein, supporting the increased protein requirements of pregnancy (an additional 25 grams daily during the second and third trimesters).

Protein is essential for fetal muscle development, placental growth, expanded blood volume, and amniotic fluid production.

**Vitamin D and calcium metabolism**: Salmon contains naturally occurring vitamin D (approximately 450-570 IU per 3-ounce serving), contributing to the 600 IU daily requirement during pregnancy. Vitamin D regulates calcium absorption and is critical for fetal bone development, immune function, and preventing pregnancy complications such as gestational diabetes and preeclampsia.

**Selenium and thyroid function**: Salmon provides selenium, a trace mineral essential for thyroid function and antioxidant defense. A single 3-ounce serving provides approximately 36-54 mcg of selenium—nearly 50% of the daily requirement—supporting maternal thyroid health and fetal thyroid hormone metabolism.

## How to Safely Eat

> **Warning:** Raw salmon (sushi, sashimi, poke) is NOT safe during pregnancy due to parasite and bacteria risk. All salmon must be fully cooked — no exceptions, even for sushi-grade. Salmon During Pregnancy

Salmon is inherently safe during pregnancy when properly selected, stored, and cooked. The main food safety consideration involves cooking temperature to eliminate any potential bacterial contamination.

**Cooking temperature guidelines**:
The FDA recommends cooking salmon to an internal temperature of 145°F (63°C), which eliminates pathogenic bacteria including Listeria, Salmonella, and harmful E. coli strains. Guidelines suggest measuring internal temperature at the thickest part of the salmon fillet using an instant-read thermometer. The flesh should be opaque throughout, with no translucent (raw-looking) centers.

**Cooking methods and times**:
- **Baking**: Bake at 400°F (205°C) for 12-15 minutes for a 3-ounce fillet
- **Pan-frying**: Cook over medium-high heat for 4-5 minutes per side (for a 1-inch thick fillet)
- **Grilling**: Cook over medium heat for 4-6 minutes per side
- **Poaching**: Simmer in broth at 160-170°F (71-77°C) for 10-12 minutes
- **Steaming**: Steam for 10-15 minutes depending on thickness

All methods are safe provided internal temperature reaches 145°F (63°C).

**Salmon selection guidance**:
Both wild-caught and farmed salmon are safe during pregnancy. Wild salmon typically contains slightly more omega-3 fatty acids, while farmed salmon is generally more affordable and consistent in availability.

Frozen salmon (flash-frozen at peak freshness) provides equivalent nutrition to fresh salmon and eliminates parasite risk through the freezing process. Guidelines suggest purchasing salmon from reputable sources where proper food handling practices are followed.

**Storage and handling**:
ACOG recommends storing raw salmon in the coldest part of the refrigerator (32-40°F or 0-4°C) and using it within 1-2 days of purchase. Frozen salmon can be safely stored for 2-3 months.

Thaw frozen salmon in the refrigerator overnight rather than at room temperature to prevent bacterial growth. Use separate cutting boards for raw salmon and other foods to prevent cross-contamination.

**Frequency guidelines**:
The FDA advises consuming 2-3 servings (8-12 ounces total) of low-mercury fish per week during pregnancy. Salmon is classified as a low-mercury fish, making it appropriate for this full serving recommendation. Pregnant individuals can safely consume salmon 2-3 times weekly without approaching any mercury exposure limits.

**DHA supplementation comparison**:
While DHA supplements are available, obtaining DHA from whole food sources like salmon provides additional complementary nutrients (protein, selenium, vitamin D) not present in supplements. ACOG recommends prioritizing dietary sources of DHA before considering supplementation, making salmon consumption a preferred approach.

**Iron and other minerals**:
Salmon provides approximately 0.5-0.8 mg of iron per serving—modest compared to red meat but bioavailable due to salmon's heme iron content. The combination of salmon's minerals (potassium, phosphorus, magnesium) supports the expanded circulatory demands of pregnancy.

## Trimester-Specific Considerations

**First Trimester (Weeks 1-13):**
Early pregnancy establishes foundational neural development, though major brain growth accelerates later. Salmon consumption beginning in the first trimester ensures consistent DHA availability throughout pregnancy. Morning sickness may make fish unappealing; gentle cooking methods like poaching or baking in mild preparations may be more tolerable than heavily seasoned options.

**Second Trimester (Weeks 14-27):**
DHA accumulation in fetal tissues accelerates significantly during this trimester. Guidelines suggest maintaining consistent salmon intake of 2-3 servings weekly to optimize DHA availability during this critical window of fetal brain development. Energy and appetite typically improve during the second trimester, making regular salmon consumption more feasible.

**Third Trimester (Weeks 28-40):**
Brain development continues intensifying through delivery, and DHA intake remains important. The final weeks of pregnancy represent a crucial period for fetal neural development, and continued salmon consumption supports cognitive development through birth. Some research suggests DHA accumulation accelerates even in the final weeks of pregnancy.

## FAQ

> **Bottom Line:** Eat salmon 2-3 times per week, cooked to 145°F. It's one of the most beneficial pregnancy foods — high omega-3, low mercury, excellent protein. Just skip the sashimi.

**Q: Is farmed salmon safe during pregnancy?**

A: Yes. Farmed salmon is safe and nutritious during pregnancy. Both wild-caught and farmed salmon contain low mercury levels and high DHA content. Farmed salmon tends to be more affordable and consistently available. Some research indicates farmed salmon contains slightly higher omega-6 content, but the overall nutritional benefit remains excellent (Oken E et al., Am J Obstet Gynecol 2005).

**Q: Can I eat salmon sushi or ceviche during pregnancy?**

A: No. Raw or undercooked salmon poses risks from parasites and bacteria such as Listeria. Salmon should be cooked to 145°F (63°C) internal temperature. However, cooked salmon sushi rolls (such as those featuring baked salmon or salmon tempura) are safe.

**Q: How much DHA do I need during pregnancy?**

A: Current guidelines suggest 200-300 mg of DHA daily during pregnancy, with additional DHA beneficial for the third trimester. A single 3-ounce serving of salmon (1,480 mg DHA) exceeds daily recommendations, meaning even one serving weekly provides substantial DHA intake.

**Q: What if I don't like salmon flavor?**

A: Other low-mercury fish providing good DHA content include trout, sardines, and herring. If these options are also unappealing, a prenatal DHA supplement (algae-based or fish oil, 200-300 mg daily) can support DHA intake when whole food consumption is difficult.

**Q: Is smoked salmon safe during pregnancy?**

A: Smoked salmon that has been fully cooked through the smoking process is safe. However, some "cold-smoked" or lightly smoked products may not reach temperatures high enough to eliminate Listeria risk. Guidelines suggest verifying that smoked salmon has been heated to 165°F (74°C) during processing, or asking the source about their smoking temperature protocols.
