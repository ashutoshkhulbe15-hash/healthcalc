# BMR Calculator: Calculate Your Basal Metabolic Rate

*Written by the ProHealthIt Editorial Team | Last updated: April 2026 | Sources cited below*

Even if you spent the entire day in bed, doing absolutely nothing, your body would still burn a significant number of calories. Your heart beats, your lungs expand, your cells divide — all of it costs energy. That baseline energy cost is your **BMR calculator** helps you understand exactly how much energy your body consumes at complete rest, providing a foundation for nutrition planning, weight management, and fitness goals.

Whether you're trying to lose weight, build muscle, or simply understand your metabolism, knowing your basal metabolic rate is a critical first step. Most people have no idea how many calories they're burning while sleeping or sitting still, yet this number forms the foundation of every calorie-based nutrition plan.

## What Is Basal Metabolic Rate?

Your basal metabolic rate (BMR) represents the number of calories your body burns in a 24-hour period while at complete rest. This includes the energy required for essential physiological functions: maintaining body temperature, powering your cardiovascular system, producing hormones, synthesizing proteins, and performing cellular respiration.

BMR accounts for 60-70% of your total daily energy expenditure in a sedentary person. For more active individuals, BMR makes up a smaller percentage, but it remains your metabolic baseline. Understanding this number is essential because it's the starting point for calculating [how many calories you need daily (TDEE)](/fitness/tdee-calculator).

Several factors influence your BMR:

- **Age**: BMR declines approximately 1-2% per decade after age 30, primarily due to the natural loss of muscle mass that occurs with aging.
- **Sex**: Men typically have higher BMRs than women of the same age and weight, largely because men naturally carry more muscle mass.
- **Height and Weight**: Larger bodies require more energy to maintain basic functions.
- **Muscle Mass**: Muscle tissue is metabolically active and requires more calories to maintain than fat tissue, even at rest.
- **Genetics**: Your genetic blueprint can account for 200-300 calorie variations in BMR, even among people with identical measurements.
- **Thyroid Function**: The thyroid gland regulates metabolic rate; an overactive thyroid increases BMR, while an underactive thyroid decreases it.
- **Medications**: Certain medications can suppress or elevate metabolic rate.

It's crucial to distinguish between BMR and TDEE (total daily energy expenditure). Many people confuse these terms. BMR is strictly the calories burned at complete rest. TDEE includes your BMR plus activity, exercise, and the thermic effect of food (calories burned digesting meals). Understanding the difference between BMR and TDEE is essential for nutrition planning.

## How to Use This Calculator

Using a BMR calculator is straightforward:

1. **Enter Your Age**: Select your current age in years.
2. **Choose Your Sex**: BMR equations differ slightly between men and women.
3. **Input Your Height**: Enter your height in inches or centimeters.
4. **Input Your Weight**: Enter your current weight in pounds or kilograms.
5. **Optional - Select Formula**: Most calculators allow you to choose between different BMR formulas. If you're unsure, the Mifflin-St Jeor formula is most commonly recommended.
6. **Get Your Result**: The calculator will display your estimated BMR in calories.

Your BMR tells you roughly how many calories you burn each day from basic metabolism alone. However, this number is only the foundation for nutrition planning. To determine how many calories you actually need daily, you must multiply your BMR by an activity factor, or use a [TDEE calculator](/fitness/tdee-calculator) that does this automatically.

A common mistake is using BMR for nutrition planning. Some people eat exactly at their BMR and wonder why they're not losing weight or building muscle — it's because they're not accounting for the calories burned through activity and digestion. Your actual calorie needs are significantly higher than your BMR (unless you're truly bedridden).

## Three BMR Formulas Compared

Researchers have developed several formulas to estimate BMR, each with different accuracy levels and use cases. The three most widely used formulas are compared below:

| **Formula** | **Year Developed** | **Equation Complexity** | **Best For** | **Typical Accuracy** |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Mifflin-St Jeor** | 1990 | Uses age, sex, height, weight | General population, most people | ±10% |
| **Harris-Benedict** | 1919 (revised 1984) | Uses age, sex, height, weight | Historical reference, still popular | ±10-15% |
| **Katch-McArdle** | 1996 | Uses lean body mass, not total weight | Individuals who know body fat % | ±5-10% if accurate body fat data |

### Mifflin-St Jeor Formula (1990)

The Mifflin-St Jeor formula is considered the gold standard for BMR estimation in modern research. Developed by researchers at the Saint Louis University School of Medicine, this equation demonstrates superior accuracy across diverse populations (Mifflin et al., 1990).

**For Men:**
BMR = (10 × weight in kg) + (6.25 × height in cm) - (5 × age in years) + 5

**For Women:**
BMR = (10 × weight in kg) + (6.25 × height in cm) - (5 × age in years) - 161

Studies show this formula typically predicts BMR within ±10% of measured values, making it reliable for nutrition planning. Most ProHealthIt users benefit from starting with Mifflin-St Jeor because it applies well to people with varying activity levels and body compositions.

### Harris-Benedict Formula (1919, revised 1984)

The Harris-Benedict formula is one of the oldest and most established equations in metabolic science. Despite being nearly a century old, it remains widely used in clinical and fitness settings (Harris & Benedict, 1919).

The original 1919 equation slightly overestimates BMR in modern populations. The 1984 revision by Roza and Shizgal provides better accuracy for contemporary populations with higher average weights.

**Original (1919) - For Men:**
BMR = 66 + (13.7 × weight in kg) + (5 × height in cm) - (6.8 × age in years)

**Original (1919) - For Women:**
BMR = 655 + (9.6 × weight in kg) + (1.8 × height in cm) - (4.7 × age in years)

This formula tends to overestimate BMR by 5-15% compared to measured values, so it may overpredict your actual calorie burn.

### Katch-McArdle Formula (1996)

The Katch-McArdle formula differs fundamentally from the other two because it uses lean body mass instead of total weight. This makes it more accurate for individuals with known body fat percentages but less useful for people without body composition data (Katch et al., 1996).

**Formula:**
BMR = 370 + (21.6 × lean body mass in kg)

To use this formula, you need to know your body fat percentage. You can estimate this using a [body fat calculator](/fitness/body-fat-calculator). If you're accurately tracking body composition, Katch-McArdle can provide exceptional precision (±5-10%), but it requires accurate body fat data to be useful.

### Which Formula Should You Use?

For most people, the Mifflin-St Jeor formula offers the best balance of accuracy and accessibility. You don't need body composition data, and it's validated across diverse populations and activity levels.

Choose Harris-Benedict if you're interested in historical reference or cross-checking your results with a widely recognized older standard.

Choose Katch-McArdle if you regularly track your body fat percentage through methods like DEXA scans, water displacement, or professional bioelectrical impedance analysis.

Our BMR calculator defaults to Mifflin-St Jeor based on extensive validation research showing its superior performance in predicting actual metabolic rate across the general population (Frankenfield et al., 2005).

## What Affects Your BMR

Understanding the factors that influence your BMR helps explain why two people of the same height and weight can have different metabolic rates.

### Age and Metabolic Decline

Metabolic rate declines approximately 1-2% per decade after age 30. This decline correlates strongly with the natural loss of muscle mass that occurs with aging, known as sarcopenia. A 40-year-old person typically has a 5-10% lower BMR than they did at age 25, assuming no change in muscle mass. This is one reason why maintaining muscle becomes increasingly important for weight management in midlife and beyond.

### Muscle Mass: The Metabolic Multiplier

Muscle tissue is metabolically expensive. Each pound of muscle tissue requires approximately 6 calories per day to maintain, while each pound of fat tissue requires only 2 calories per day. Someone with 30% body fat and someone with 20% body fat at the same weight can have significantly different BMRs. The person with more muscle mass burns more calories at rest.

This is why understanding your body composition and incorporating resistance training into your fitness routine can meaningfully increase your BMR.

### Sex and Hormonal Differences

Men typically have 5-10% higher BMRs than women of the same age and weight. This difference exists primarily because men naturally carry more muscle mass and less body fat. However, this is a statistical average; individual variation is substantial. Hormonal fluctuations during the menstrual cycle, pregnancy, and menopause also influence metabolic rate.

### Genetics and Individual Variation

Your genes influence metabolic rate through multiple pathways: the number and efficiency of mitochondria (the cellular structures that produce energy), thyroid hormone sensitivity, and the distribution of fast-twitch versus slow-twitch muscle fibers. Twin studies suggest genetics account for 20-30% of BMR variation.

This means two people with identical measurements might have BMRs that differ by 200-300 calories per day. Your calculator provides an estimate based on population averages, but your actual BMR may vary.

### Thyroid Function

The thyroid gland produces hormones that regulate metabolic rate. An overactive thyroid (hyperthyroidism) can increase BMR by 30-40%, while an underactive thyroid (hypothyroidism) can decrease it by 20-30%. If you have thyroid dysfunction, your actual BMR may differ significantly from calculator estimates.

### Medications

Some medications influence metabolic rate. Stimulants like amphetamines and some ADHD medications increase BMR. Certain antidepressants, beta-blockers, and hormonal medications can decrease it.

## BMR vs TDEE — What's the Difference?

One of the most important nutrition concepts is distinguishing between BMR and TDEE. These terms are related but represent very different numbers.

**BMR (Basal Metabolic Rate)** = Calories burned at complete rest, 24 hours per day, in a fasted state.

**TDEE (Total Daily Energy Expenditure)** = Your BMR + calories burned through activity + thermic effect of food (calories burned during digestion).

For a sedentary person, TDEE might be only 20-30% higher than BMR. For an active person exercising 5+ days per week, TDEE might be 50-100% higher than BMR.

**Example:**
- Sarah's BMR = 1,400 calories
- Sarah exercises 3 days per week (moderate activity)
- Sarah's TDEE = approximately 2,000 calories

If Sarah only eats at her BMR (1,400 calories), she'd create a 600-calorie deficit daily, which is too aggressive. But if she eats at her TDEE (2,000 calories), she maintains her weight. To lose weight, she'd eat 1,700 calories (500-calorie deficit), not restrict to BMR.

This is why [calculating your TDEE](/fitness/tdee-calculator) is essential for effective nutrition planning. BMR is the foundation, but it's not the number you use for daily calorie targets.

Additionally, understanding your BMR helps inform other fitness calculations. Once you know your BMR and TDEE, you can use a [calorie deficit calculator](/fitness/calorie-deficit-calculator) to determine appropriate calorie targets for your goal, whether that's weight loss, muscle gain, or maintenance.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is a normal BMR?

BMR varies widely based on age, sex, height, weight, and muscle mass. General ranges:
- **Adult women**: 1,200-1,600 calories/day
- **Adult men**: 1,600-2,200 calories/day

Larger individuals and those with more muscle mass have higher BMRs. Smaller individuals and those with higher body fat percentages have lower BMRs. A BMR within this range is considered normal.

### How accurate is a BMR calculator?

The Mifflin-St Jeor formula typically predicts BMR within ±10% of measured values. This means if your calculated BMR is 1,500 calories, your actual BMR is likely between 1,350-1,650 calories.

Accuracy decreases if you have unusual body composition (very high muscle mass or very high body fat), genetic metabolic variations, or medical conditions affecting metabolism. Direct measurement through indirect calorimetry is more accurate but requires expensive lab equipment.

For nutrition planning, calculator estimates are accurate enough to guide calorie targets. You can adjust based on results over time.

### Can I increase my BMR?

Yes, though the changes are modest. The most effective approach is building muscle mass through resistance training. Adding five pounds of muscle increases BMR by approximately 30 calories per day.

Other approaches with smaller effects include consistent aerobic exercise, maintaining adequate protein intake, getting sufficient sleep, managing stress, and staying hydrated. However, these don't dramatically change BMR — they're more valuable for overall health.

Crash dieting and severe calorie restriction actually decrease BMR by 10-20% as your body enters conservation mode, making weight loss progressively harder.

### Should I eat at my BMR to lose weight?

No. Eating at your BMR creates too large a calorie deficit for most people and can slow metabolism, suppress hormones, and make sustained weight loss harder. Use your TDEE to calculate an appropriate deficit (typically 300-500 calories below TDEE for sustainable weight loss), not your BMR.

### Does BMR change with age?

Yes. BMR declines about 1-2% per decade after age 30, primarily due to muscle mass loss. A 50-year-old with the same weight as their 30-year-old self will have a lower BMR. This is one reason maintaining muscle mass becomes increasingly important with age.
