# Keto Macros: How to Calculate Your Macronutrient Targets

*Written by the ProHealthIt Editorial Team | Sources cited below*

---

## Introduction

The ketogenic diet's effectiveness hinges on precise macronutrient ratios that keep the body in a fat-burning metabolic state called ketosis. Unlike low-fat diets that rely primarily on calorie reduction, keto works by shifting the body's fuel source from carbohydrates to fats and ketones. This guide walks through the science behind keto macros, how to calculate your personal targets, and how to track them effectively for best results.

## What Are Keto Macros?

Macronutrients (macros) are the three nutrients that provide calories: fat, protein, and carbohydrates. The ketogenic diet uses a specific ratio of these three to trigger ketosis, where the liver produces ketone bodies from fat for energy.

The standard ketogenic diet maintains approximately:

- **70% of calories from fat**
- **25% of calories from protein**
- **5% of calories from carbohydrates**

This ratio forces the body to deplete its glycogen stores within 3–7 days and shift to burning fat for fuel.

## Standard Keto Macro Ratios

| **Calorie Level** | **Total Calories** | **Fat (70%)** | **Protein (25%)** | **Carbs (5%)** |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Low (Weight Loss)** | 1,500 cal | 117g | 94g | 19g |
| **Moderate** | 2,000 cal | 156g | 125g | 25g |
| **Maintenance** | 2,500 cal | 194g | 156g | 31g |
| **High (Muscle Gain)** | 3,000 cal | 233g | 188g | 38g |
| **Very High (Athletes)** | 3,500 cal | 272g | 219g | 44g |

**Note:** These are approximate; individual needs can vary based on activity level, body composition goals, and metabolism. The macro targets above assume:
- 4 calories per gram of protein
- 4 calories per gram of carbohydrate
- 9 calories per gram of fat

Practical calculations typically round macros to the nearest 5g for easier tracking.

## Step-by-Step Keto Macro Calculation

### Step 1: Determine Your Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE)

Your TDEE is the total calories burned in a day. Calculate it using:

**TDEE = BMR × Activity Factor**

**Example:** 35-year-old woman, 5'6", 160 lbs, moderately active (exercises 4×/week)

Using the Mifflin-St Jeor equation:
- BMR = (10 × 72.6 kg) + (6.25 × 168 cm) − (5 × 35) − 161 = 1,527 calories
- TDEE = 1,527 × 1.55 (activity factor) = **2,367 calories**

For simplicity, round to 2,350 calories.

### Step 2: Choose a Calorie Deficit or Surplus

For **weight loss:** Subtract 300–500 calories from TDEE
- Example: 2,350 − 400 = 1,950 calories/day target

For **maintenance:** Use TDEE as-is
- Example: 2,350 calories/day

For **muscle gain:** Add 300–500 calories to TDEE
- Example: 2,350 + 400 = 2,750 calories/day target

### Step 3: Calculate Macro Grams

Using the 70/25/5 ratio:

**Step 3a: Protein (25% of calories)**
- Calories from protein = target calories × 0.25
- Grams of protein = (calories from protein) ÷ 4
- **Example:** 1,950 × 0.25 = 487 ÷ 4 = **122g protein**

**Step 3b: Fat (70% of calories)**
- Calories from fat = target calories × 0.70
- Grams of fat = (calories from fat) ÷ 9
- **Example:** 1,950 × 0.70 = 1,365 ÷ 9 = **152g fat**

**Step 3c: Carbohydrates (5% of calories)**
- Calories from carbs = target calories × 0.05
- Grams of carbs = (calories from carbs) ÷ 4
- **Example:** 1,950 × 0.05 = 97 ÷ 4 = **24g carbohydrates**

### Step 4: Verify Your Math

Add up calories: (122 × 4) + (152 × 9) + (24 × 4) = 488 + 1,368 + 96 = **1,952 calories** ✓

### Complete Example Calculation

**Person:** Male, age 32, 5'10", 200 lbs, lightly active (3 workouts/week)
**Goal:** Weight loss

1. **Calculate BMR:** (10 × 90.9 kg) + (6.25 × 178 cm) − (5 × 32) + 5 = 1,717 calories
2. **Calculate TDEE:** 1,717 × 1.375 = 2,361 calories
3. **Choose deficit:** 2,361 − 450 = 1,911 calories/day target
4. **Protein (25%):** 1,911 × 0.25 = 478 ÷ 4 = **120g protein**
5. **Fat (70%):** 1,911 × 0.70 = 1,338 ÷ 9 = **149g fat**
6. **Carbs (5%):** 1,911 × 0.05 = 96 ÷ 4 = **24g carbohydrates**

**Daily macro targets: 120g protein | 149g fat | 24g carbs**

## Net Carbs vs Total Carbs

On keto, the distinction between total carbs and net carbs is crucial for tracking carbohydrate impact.

**Total Carbohydrates** = all carbs, including fiber, sugar alcohols, and digestible carbs

**Net Carbohydrates** = total carbs − fiber − sugar alcohols

The body digests net carbs; fiber and most sugar alcohols pass through largely unabsorbed. For ketosis maintenance, net carbs are what matters.

**Calculation Example:**
- Item: 1 medium avocado (raw)
- Total carbs: 12g
- Fiber: 10g
- Net carbs: 12 − 10 = **2g net carbs**

This is why avocados and non-starchy vegetables are keto-friendly despite modest carb counts.

**Note:** Sugar alcohols (erythritol, xylitol) vary in impact. Erythritol has minimal metabolic effect; maltitol raises blood sugar more significantly. Read labels carefully.

For keto macro tracking, most practitioners count **net carbs** toward the daily 20–50g limit, though some individuals respond better to stricter total carb counts. Experiment to find your personal threshold for ketosis.

## Signs You're In Ketosis

Ketosis typically develops within 3–7 days of maintaining keto macros, though timing varies individually. Common indicators include:

**Appetite Suppression:** Ketones signal satiety through hormonal changes; many people eat less without hunger.[^1]

**Mental Clarity ("Keto Brain"):** Some report enhanced focus, especially after the 2–3 week adaptation period when the brain fully switches to ketone fuel.

**Metallic Taste or Fruity Breath:** Acetone (a ketone body) is exhaled, producing a distinctive taste or odor.

**Fatigue (Initial):** The first 1–2 weeks often involve energy dips while the body adapts—"keto flu"—resolved with electrolytes and adequate sleep.

**Increased Urination:** Ketones are water-soluble and excreted through urine; increased frequency may occur temporarily.

**Objective Markers:**
- Blood ketone meters measure beta-hydroxybutyrate (most accurate)
- Urine strips measure ketones (less reliable as adaptation progresses; kidney excretion changes)
- Breath meters measure acetone (moderate accuracy)

Most people don't need to test—consistent adherence to keto macros induces ketosis reliably.

## Common Keto Macro Mistakes

### 1. Not Tracking Calories

Many assume keto's hormonal benefits eliminate calorie counting necessity. While keto does suppress appetite and optimize hormones, the laws of thermodynamics still apply.[^2] Excess calories, even from keto-friendly foods, prevent weight loss.

**Solution:** Track food intake for at least the first 2–3 weeks to establish awareness. After adaptation, some can eat intuitively; others benefit from ongoing monitoring.

### 2. Too Much Protein

Excessive protein is converted to glucose via gluconeogenesis, potentially disrupting ketosis. However, moderate protein (25% of calories) preserves muscle and maintains satiety without this issue.

**Solution:** Aim for 0.7–1.0g per pound of body weight, not more. Too little protein risks muscle loss; too much risks exiting ketosis.

### 3. Miscounting Net Carbs

Underestimating carbs—through misreading labels, not accounting for fiber, or consuming "keto" products with hidden sugars—is the most common reason people fail to achieve ketosis.

**Solution:** Weigh food and verify labels. Use apps like Cronometer that default to net carb counting. Be skeptical of packaged "keto" products; they may contain sugar alcohols that impact blood sugar individually.

### 4. Neglecting Electrolytes

Ketosis increases sodium and magnesium excretion. Inadequate intake causes headaches, fatigue, muscle cramps, and "keto flu" symptoms.

**Solution:** Consume 3,000–5,000 mg sodium daily (salt foods appropriately), 300–400 mg magnesium (nuts, seeds, dark leafy greens), and 3,000–4,000 mg potassium (avocados, leafy greens, bone broth).

### 5. Overeating High-Calorie Fats

Nuts, oils, and cheese are calorie-dense. It's easy to exceed calorie targets while tracking only macros, especially when nuts provide minimal satiety.

**Solution:** Weigh portions of oils (1 Tbsp = 120 calories), nuts (1 oz almonds = 164 calories), and cheese. Prioritize whole foods and satiating fats (avocado, fatty fish, eggs) over processed options.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I use a different macro ratio (e.g., 80/15/5)?**
A: Yes. Adjusted ratios exist for specific goals (e.g., targeted keto for athletes, cyclic keto for high-volume training). However, the 70/25/5 standard is research-validated for sustainable ketosis and body composition changes.[^3] Experiment only after mastering the baseline.

**Q: How strict are the macro ratios?**
A: ±5% is normal variation; the body adapts. Staying within 5–10% error range maintains ketosis. Beyond that, some individuals fall out of ketosis or experience reduced benefits.

**Q: Should I adjust macros as my weight changes?**
A: Yes. Recalculate TDEE every 10–15 lbs lost, as metabolic needs decrease with weight. A 180 lb person's TDEE differs from a 160 lb person's; calories should scale proportionally.

**Q: Is intermittent fasting compatible with keto macros?**
A: Yes. Keto's appetite suppression makes IF easier. Maintain macro targets during your eating window; don't increase calories to compensate for fasting.

**Q: What if I want to prioritize muscle gain on keto?**
A: Use moderate calories above TDEE (+300–500 cal), increase protein to 1.0–1.2g per lb, and perform progressive resistance training 3–5×/week. This approach (often called "ketogains") preserves muscle while building strength. Ensure adequate sleep and consider timing protein intake around workouts to support anabolic processes and recovery.

## Keto Food Examples by Macro Profile

**High-Fat, Moderate-Protein Foods (Satiating):**
- Avocado (12g fat, 3g protein per medium fruit)
- Fatty fish (salmon: 13g fat, 25g protein per 3 oz)
- Full-fat cheese (9g fat, 7g protein per oz)
- Eggs (5g fat, 6g protein per large egg)
- Nuts and seeds (14g fat, 5g protein per oz almonds)

**Low-Carb Vegetables (Fiber-Rich):**
- Leafy greens: spinach, kale, lettuce (1–2g net carb per cup)
- Cruciferous: broccoli, cauliflower, cabbage (3–5g net carb per cup)
- Zucchini noodles (3g net carb per cup)
- Bell peppers (3g net carb per pepper, small)

**Foods to Avoid (High Carb or Processed):**
- Grains: bread, pasta, rice, cereal
- Most fruits (except berries: ~7g net carb per cup)
- Sugar and sweets
- Vegetable oils high in linoleic acid (corn, soy—use olive, avocado, butter instead)

## Key Takeaways

- **Standard keto macros:** 70% fat, 25% protein, 5% carbs (often 20–50g net carbs daily)
- **Calculate personalised targets** using TDEE and your calorie goal
- **Net carbs matter more than total carbs** for ketosis; subtract fiber
- **Electrolytes are essential**; don't skip sodium, magnesium, and potassium
- **Track intake initially** to ensure accuracy; scale as adaptation occurs
- **Recalculate quarterly or after 10–15 lb changes** in body weight
