# Can I Eat Sardines During Pregnancy? Safety Guide

**Quick Answer:** ✅ **Safe** — Sardines are very low in mercury, exceptionally high in omega-3 fatty acids (DHA and EPA) and calcium, making them one of the most nutrient-dense pregnancy foods available.

*Written by the ProHealthIt Editorial Team | Sources cited below*

---

## The Short Answer

Research shows that children whose mothers consumed adequate omega-3 during pregnancy have measurably higher cognitive scores, better visual development, and superior behavioral outcomes into childhood. Sardines deliver the highest amount of brain-critical DHA per serving of nearly any fish—400-500 mg in just three ounces, exceeding the recommended 200-300 mg daily target. Sardines are also exceptionally low in mercury (among the lowest of all fish), making them safe multiple times weekly without accumulation concerns. As a bonus, the edible sardine bones provide 325-400 mg of highly bioavailable calcium per serving—contributing substantially toward pregnancy's 1,000 mg daily requirement. The combination of safety, omega-3 concentration, and calcium density makes sardines one of the most nutrient-strategic foods available during pregnancy. Fresh or canned sardines are equally nutritious and safe.

---

## Why Sardines Matter During Pregnancy

DHA deserves particular emphasis in pregnancy discussions, as it directly impacts fetal brain development. The brain accumulates DHA rapidly during the third trimester and first years of life; approximately 8% of brain dry weight consists of DHA. Fetal brain DHA content correlates directly with maternal DHA intake during pregnancy, with studies demonstrating that children born to mothers with adequate DHA intake show measurably better cognitive performance, improved visual development, and superior behavioral outcomes through childhood. The American Academy of Pediatrics recommends pregnant and lactating women consume 200-300mg daily DHA specifically (beyond broader omega-3 recommendations) due to this critical developmental role.

EPA, the other primary omega-3 fatty acid in sardines, supports maternal cardiovascular health and inflammatory balance. Pregnancy naturally increases inflammatory markers and blood clotting; EPA's anti-inflammatory properties moderate these pregnancy-related changes, reducing preeclampsia risk in some studies and supporting overall maternal wellbeing. EPA also supports maternal mental health; postpartum depression rates decline in populations with higher omega-3 intake.

Calcium in sardines deserves special attention because edible sardine bones provide highly bioavailable calcium in its most absorbable form. Pregnancy increases calcium demands by 25% despite already-elevated baseline requirements (1,000mg daily), as the fetus accumulates 200-300 grams of calcium during pregnancy specifically for skeletal development. If maternal calcium intake is insufficient, fetal calcium accumulation proceeds regardless, drawing calcium from maternal skeleton and potentially increasing maternal osteoporosis risk decades later. Sardine consumption addresses this dual need: supporting fetal calcium accumulation while protecting maternal bone density.

Sardines also provide iron (approximately 2-3mg per 3-ounce serving), addressing pregnancy's 27mg daily iron requirement (nearly double non-pregnant needs). The iron in fish is heme iron, the most bioavailable form, meaning sardine iron absorbs more efficiently than iron from plant sources or supplements. Combined with vitamin C in acidic sauces or tomato preparations, sardine iron absorption becomes optimal.

## How to Safely Consume Sardines

Canned sardines in water or oil represent the most convenient pregnancy option, requiring minimal preparation while providing full nutritional benefits. The canning process involves heating sardines to temperatures well exceeding the 145°F food safety requirement, eliminating any foodborne pathogen risk. Simply transfer canned sardines to a plate, remove any large bones if desired (though smaller bones are entirely safe and nutritious), and consume as-is or on whole grain crackers, in salads, or as a protein addition to meals. This extreme convenience supports consistent consumption across pregnancy's duration.

Canned sardines packed in water contain fewer calories (approximately 100 per 3.75-ounce can) compared to oil-packed varieties (approximately 150-200 calories), though oil-packed sardines provide additional vitamin E and palatability benefits. Both are equally safe and nutritious during pregnancy; choose based on personal preference and dietary goals. Avoid sardines packed in sauces high in sodium; plain water or lightly salted oil-packed varieties offer superior nutrition.

Fresh sardines require the same 145°F cooking temperature as other fish but provide superior sensory experience for some pregnant people. Grill fresh sardines whole at medium-high heat for 3-4 minutes per side until the flesh becomes opaque. Pan-sear in minimal oil for similar duration. Bake whole sardines at 400°F (204°C) for 10-12 minutes. Verify internal temperature with a meat thermometer inserted into the thickest part of the fish. Properly cooked fresh sardines develop appealing crispy skin while maintaining tender flesh inside.

When consuming sardines with edible bones, no special preparation is required: simply eat the small bones alongside fish flesh. These bones present no choking risk and provide exceptional calcium benefit—approximately 100mg additional calcium per 3-ounce serving from bone consumption. For those uncomfortable consuming visible bones, canned sardines are sometimes deboned before canning, though whole-fish versions provide superior nutrition.

Sardines pair excellently with pregnancy-supportive ingredients: tomato sauce (adding vitamin C for iron absorption), olive oil, lemon juice, whole grains, and leafy vegetables. Mediterranean preparations with capers, olives, and herbs complement sardine flavor while adding micronutrient diversity. Asian preparations with ginger and scallions provide similar benefits with different flavor profiles.

## Nutritional Benefits of Sardines

| Omega-3 (EPA+DHA) | 400-500mg | 200-300mg | 133-250% |
| Calcium | 325-400mg | 1,000mg | 33-40% |
| Protein | 22-24g | 71g | 31% |

| Selenium | 35mcg | 60mcg | 58% |
| Vitamin D | 200-400 IU | 600 IU | 33-67% |
| Mercury | <0.013 ppm | <0.1 ppm | Safe |

Sardines' omega-3 density distinguishes them among commonly consumed foods. A single 3-ounce serving of canned sardines provides 400-500mg EPA+DHA, exceeding the daily recommendation within a single serving. This exceptional concentration means a pregnant person consuming sardines twice weekly accumulates approximately 900-1,400mg omega-3 per week—nearly double the weekly recommendation. This margin of safety is particularly valuable for pregnant people uncertain about dietary consistency or those managing other dietary restrictions limiting omega-3 sources.

Calcium content in sardines (including edible bones) reaches 325-400mg per 3-ounce serving, providing approximately one-third of the daily pregnancy requirement in a single serving. This density becomes valuable when dietary calcium sources are limited, and the bioavailability of sardine calcium (bone matrix contains organic and mineral components optimal for absorption) exceeds calcium from supplements or fortified foods.

Selenium, of which sardines provide 35 micrograms per 3-ounce serving (58% of the daily adequate intake), supports thyroid peroxidase—the enzyme essential for thyroid hormone synthesis. Adequate selenium intake ensures maternal thyroid function remains robust during pregnancy's increased hormonal demands, with cascading benefits for fetal metabolic development.

## Trimester-Specific Considerations

**First Trimester:** Early pregnancy neurological development makes omega-3 intake particularly important, even before many pregnant people discover pregnancy. DHA accumulates in fetal brain tissue throughout pregnancy, but brain development begins in the first trimester. Introducing sardine consumption early in pregnancy establishes consistent omega-3 intake during this critical window. For pregnant people managing first trimester nausea, sardines' mild preparation (simply opening a can) represents an accessible protein option when more complex meal preparation feels overwhelming.

**Second Trimester:** Fetal growth accelerates and brain development advances rapidly during weeks 13-27. Omega-3 consumption during this period remains crucial for establishing adequate fetal DHA concentration. Calcium demands intensify as fetal skeletal development accelerates; sardine consumption (2-3 times weekly) supplies both nutrients efficiently. Second trimester often brings appetite restoration, allowing pregnant people to experiment with fresh sardine preparations if preferred over canned versions.

**Third Trimester:** Final trimester brings accelerated fetal brain growth and continued skeletal mineralization. Omega-3 and calcium demands persist at high levels through term delivery. DHA, while accumulating throughout pregnancy, shows particular importance in final weeks as fetal brain growth accelerates before birth. Continued sardine consumption (2-3 times weekly) maintains optimal micronutrient status, particularly calcium supporting continued fetal skeletal development and omega-3 supporting final brain growth phases.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Are sardines high in sodium?**
Canned sardines in saltwater or oil typically contain 300-400mg sodium per 3-ounce serving (approximately 13-17% of the 2,300mg daily sodium guideline). While this is moderate sodium content, pregnancy hypertension concerns warrant attention. Low-sodium sardine varieties exist and provide superior options for those managing blood pressure. Rinsing regular canned sardines briefly under running water reduces sodium by approximately 10-15%. For most pregnant people, moderate sardine consumption (2-3 times weekly) poses no sodium concerns; those with hypertension should select low-sodium varieties.

**Is the mercury in sardines a concern?**
No. Sardines are among the lowest-mercury fish available, with mercury content typically below 0.013 parts per million—lower than most other commonly consumed fish. This exceptionally low mercury content permits safe consumption multiple times weekly without accumulation concern. The FDA and EPA explicitly list sardines as a "Best Choice" fish, the highest safety category for pregnancy consumption.

**Can pregnant people with fish allergies eat sardines?**
No. Fish allergies create allergic responses to all fish species, including sardines. Finned fish allergy differs from shellfish allergy (which would not extend to sardines), but those with any fish allergy should avoid all fish including sardines. Alternative omega-3 sources include walnuts (2.3g ALA per ounce, a precursor to EPA/DHA), ground flaxseed (2.3g ALA per tablespoon), chia seeds (5.1g ALA per ounce), or algae-based supplements (250-500mg EPA+DHA per serving). Discuss omega-3 supplementation with your healthcare provider if food sources are unavailable.

**Canned or fresh sardines during pregnancy—which is better?**
Both canned and fresh sardines are equally safe and nutritious. Canned sardines offer superior convenience, longer shelf stability, and guaranteed proper heat treatment for pathogen elimination. Fresh sardines require careful cooking to 145°F internal temperature and more involved preparation, though they offer potentially superior sensory experience. During pregnancy, when convenience often correlates with consistency of consumption, canned sardines represent the practical choice for most pregnant people. Select varieties in water or lightly oiled options, avoiding heavily sauced versions.

**Can I consume sardine oil as an alternative to eating whole sardines?**
Sardine oil supplements provide omega-3 fatty acids but lack the calcium, iron, and complete protein sardines offer. Additionally, fish oil supplementation carries marginally increased bleeding risk in some studies, though clinical significance remains debated. Whole sardines provide comprehensive nutrition in one food; if sardine consumption feels impossible, omega-3 supplementation combined with other calcium and protein sources is preferable to sardine oil alone.
