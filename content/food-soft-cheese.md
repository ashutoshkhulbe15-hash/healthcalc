# Can I Eat Soft Cheese During Pregnancy? Safety Guide

*Written by the ProHealthIt Editorial Team | Sources cited below*

---

## Quick Answer

⚠️ **SAFE WITH CAUTION** — Soft cheeses are safe during pregnancy only if made from pasteurized milk. Most commercial US soft cheeses (brie, feta, camembert, ricotta, mozzarella) are pasteurized and safe. Always check the label to verify "made from pasteurized milk." Unpasteurized soft cheeses carry Listeria risk.

---

## The Short Answer

Many pregnancy resources paint all soft cheeses with the same warning, suggesting they're uniformly dangerous—but that's a dangerous oversimplification. The truth is that soft cheeses are perfectly safe during pregnancy if they're made from pasteurized milk. The Listeria risk that gets all the attention applies only to unpasteurized cheeses. Fortunately, most commercially produced soft cheeses in the United States are made from pasteurized milk, making them safe for pregnant individuals. However, imported soft cheeses, artisanal varieties, and cheeses from farmers markets may be unpasteurized. The key is simply checking the label for "made from pasteurized milk" and asking restaurants about their sources. When you confirm pasteurization, soft cheeses provide valuable calcium, protein, and vitamin A—nutrients essential for fetal bone development and maternal health.

## Why Soft Cheese Matters During Pregnancy

Soft cheeses are nutrient-dense foods providing calcium and protein essential for fetal skeletal development, but their safety depends entirely on pasteurization status. The nutritional benefits are substantial when the safety concern is properly managed through pasteurization verification.

**Listeria risk and immunosuppression**: Pregnancy causes specific immunosuppression of cell-mediated immunity (T-cell responses), which particularly impairs the immune response to Listeria monocytogenes. Pregnant individuals are approximately 10 times more susceptible to Listeria infection than the general population, and Listeria infection during pregnancy carries devastating consequences including miscarriage, stillbirth, premature delivery, and neonatal sepsis (CDC Listeria Prevention, 2023). Listeria is uniquely dangerous because it survives refrigeration and can multiply at cold temperatures—conditions that inhibit most other bacteria. Soft cheeses made from unpasteurized milk provide an ideal environment for Listeria multiplication.

**Pasteurization eliminates Listeria**: Heat-treatment during pasteurization (heating milk to 161°F/72°C for 15 seconds or higher temperatures for shorter durations) reliably eliminates Listeria and other pathogenic bacteria. Pasteurization does not significantly alter cheese texture, flavor, or nutritional content, making it a straightforward safety measure. When soft cheeses are made from pasteurized milk, Listeria risk is essentially eliminated.

**Calcium and fetal skeletal development**: Soft cheeses provide bioavailable calcium, with a 1-ounce serving of cheese providing approximately 200 mg of calcium—roughly 17% of the 1,000 mg daily requirement during pregnancy. Adequate calcium intake supports fetal bone mineralization, which accelerates dramatically in the third trimester. Maternal calcium depletion during pregnancy is prevented only through adequate dietary intake; if dietary calcium is insufficient, the fetus will preferentially extract calcium from maternal bones, potentially increasing long-term osteoporosis risk in the mother.

**Complete protein and amino acids**: Soft cheeses provide all nine essential amino acids in a concentrated form. A 1-ounce serving provides approximately 6-8 grams of protein, contributing to the increased 25-gram daily requirement during pregnancy. The combination of casein and whey proteins in cheese supports satiety and nutrient absorption.

**Vitamin A and fetal development**: Soft cheeses contain preformed vitamin A (retinol), essential for fetal eye development, immune system programming, and cellular differentiation. However, excessive vitamin A intake (above 10,000 IU daily) during early pregnancy poses teratogenic risk. Moderate soft cheese consumption provides beneficial vitamin A without excessive intake.

**Bioavailable micronutrients**: Soft cheeses provide readily absorbed zinc, selenium, and B vitamins including B12 and riboflavin—nutrients supporting fetal nervous system development and maternal energy metabolism.

## How to Safely Eat Soft Cheese During Pregnancy

The safety of soft cheese consumption during pregnancy depends entirely on confirming pasteurization status. When this simple verification is completed, soft cheeses are entirely safe and nutritious.

**Identifying pasteurized soft cheese**:
ACOG recommends checking the label or packaging for explicit statement "made from pasteurized milk." This language indicates the cheese was manufactured using pasteurized milk, eliminating Listeria risk. Labels may state "pasteurized" or "made from pasteurized milk"—both confirm safety.

**Common pasteurized soft cheeses** (generally safe when purchased from mainstream commercial sources):
- Brie (most commercial brands in the US)
- Feta (typically pasteurized in US commercial production)
- Camembert (usually pasteurized in commercial production)
- Ricotta (almost universally pasteurized in the US)
- Fresh mozzarella (varies by brand; verify label)
- Chèvre/goat cheese (varies significantly; verify label)
- Cream cheese (almost always pasteurized)
- Mascarpone (typically pasteurized)
- Burrata (usually pasteurized; verify label)

**Common unpasteurized soft cheeses** (to be avoided):
- Many imported French, Italian, or Spanish soft cheeses
- Artisanal or farm-made soft cheeses
- Raw milk cheese (explicitly labeled)
- Many goat cheeses from local producers

**Label verification protocol**:
FDA advises reading labels carefully, as "made with unpasteurized milk" or "raw milk cheese" explicitly identifies unsafe options. When in doubt about a cheese, the safest approach is to avoid it. When purchasing from restaurants, delis, or farmers markets, asking the vendor directly: "Is this cheese made from pasteurized milk?" provides definitive safety information.

**Cheese selection recommendations**:
Guidelines suggest that pregnant individuals purchasing soft cheeses prioritize mainstream commercial brands available in standard supermarkets, which have comprehensive safety protocols and typically use pasteurized milk. Artisanal, imported, or specialty soft cheeses require explicit label verification or vendor confirmation before consumption.

**Storage and handling**:
Guidelines suggest storing soft cheeses in the coldest section of the refrigerator (32-35°F or 0-2°C) and using them within 3-5 days of opening. Do not leave soft cheese at room temperature for more than 2 hours (or 1 hour in warm environments), as temperature abuse can promote bacterial multiplication. Use separate utensils when removing cheese from the package to prevent contamination.

## Safe Soft Cheese Options and Alternatives

Soft cheeses made from pasteurized milk are safe and provide valuable nutrition during pregnancy. However, when pasteurization status is unclear, alternatives provide equivalent nutrition.

**Safe pasteurized soft cheese options** (nutrition per 1-ounce serving):

| Cheese Type | Calcium (mg) | Protein (g) | Fat (g) | Vitamin A (IU) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Brie (pasteurized) | 190-210 | 6-7 | 8-9 | 240-280 |
| Feta (pasteurized) | 200-220 | 5-6 | 8 | 260-300 |
| Ricotta (pasteurized) | 120-140 | 3-4 | 2-3 | 100-120 |
| Fresh Mozzarella (pasteurized) | 190-210 | 6-7 | 6-7 | 200-240 |
| Cream Cheese (pasteurized) | 100-120 | 2-3 | 10 | 380-420 |

**Hard cheese alternatives** (naturally Listeria-safe, even if unpasteurized):
Hard cheeses (cheddar, parmesan, gruyere, aged gouda) are safe during pregnancy regardless of pasteurization status due to their low moisture content and long aging processes, which prevent Listeria survival. A 1-ounce serving of hard cheese provides similar calcium (190-200 mg) and protein (7-8 g) as soft cheese, with slightly higher fat content.

**Non-cheese calcium-rich alternatives** (if soft cheese is unavailable):
- Pasteurized yogurt (plain or Greek): 200-300 mg calcium per 6-8 oz serving
- Milk (pasteurized): 300 mg calcium per 8 oz serving
- Fortified plant-based milk: 300-450 mg calcium per 8 oz serving
- Pasteurized cottage cheese: 100-150 mg calcium per 1/2 cup
- Calcium-set tofu: 350-400 mg calcium per 1/2 cup
- Leafy greens (collards, kale): 100-180 mg calcium per 1 cup cooked

## Trimester-Specific Considerations

**First Trimester (Weeks 1-13):**
Early pregnancy establishes foundational development. Calcium intake is important throughout pregnancy, though maximum fetal calcium accumulation occurs in the third trimester. Verify pasteurization status of any soft cheeses consumed. Morning sickness may make cheese less appealing; alternatives like yogurt or milk provide equivalent calcium with potentially greater tolerance.

**Second Trimester (Weeks 14-27):**
Fetal bone development accelerates, and adequate calcium intake becomes increasingly important. Soft cheeses can be incorporated into meals as calcium sources, provided pasteurization is confirmed. Energy and appetite typically normalize, making regular cheese consumption feasible.

**Third Trimester (Weeks 28-40):**
The final trimester sees massive calcium accumulation in fetal bone mineral density, with the fetus accumulating approximately 250-330 mg of calcium daily. Adequate maternal dietary calcium is essential during this period to prevent mobilization of maternal bone calcium reserves. Continued soft cheese consumption (when pasteurized) provides valuable calcium support during this critical window. Some pregnant individuals experience heartburn or digestive sensitivity; lower-fat soft cheese options may be better tolerated than full-fat varieties.

## FAQ

**Q: Are all soft cheeses unsafe during pregnancy?**

A: No. Soft cheeses made from pasteurized milk are safe. The danger is specifically in soft cheeses made from unpasteurized milk. Most commercial US soft cheeses are pasteurized and safe; the challenge is verifying pasteurization status, particularly with imported, artisanal, or specialty cheeses (CDC Listeria Prevention, 2023).

**Q: What is Listeria and why is it dangerous during pregnancy?**

A: Listeria monocytogenes is a bacterium that survives in cold temperatures and can multiply in refrigerated foods. During pregnancy, cell-mediated immunity is suppressed, making Listeria infection approximately 10 times more likely and significantly more severe. Listeria can cross the placental barrier, directly infecting the fetus and potentially causing miscarriage, stillbirth, or neonatal sepsis. Early symptoms may be nonspecific (fever, muscle aches), but the infection progresses rapidly and can affect the central nervous system and bloodstream.

**Q: If I accidentally ate unpasteurized soft cheese, will my baby be okay?**

A: Consuming unpasteurized cheese once does not guarantee Listeria infection. If no symptoms develop within 4-6 weeks, infection is unlikely. However, if you develop fever, muscle aches, headache, confusion, or any concerning symptoms, contact your healthcare provider immediately and mention the unpasteurized cheese exposure. Prompt treatment of Listeria infection can prevent serious fetal complications (FDA Food Safety for Pregnant Women, 2023).

**Q: Are soft cheeses from restaurants safe?**

A: Restaurants in the United States are required to use pasteurized dairy products for soft cheeses on standard menus. However, specialty restaurants, international restaurants, or cheeseboards featuring imported cheeses may not follow this requirement. When uncertain, asking the restaurant staff directly about pasteurization status is appropriate and encouraged.

**Q: Is all goat cheese unsafe during pregnancy?**

A: Goat cheese safety depends on pasteurization, not the goat milk source. Commercially produced goat cheese (chèvre) from mainstream brands is typically pasteurized, but artisanal or farm-made goat cheese is often unpasteurized. Always verify the label or ask the vendor about pasteurization status before consuming goat cheese.

**Q: How much soft cheese is safe to eat?**

A: No quantity limit exists for pasteurized soft cheese. Dairy recommendations suggest 3 servings of dairy daily during pregnancy (one serving = 1 cup milk equivalent, including 1-1.5 ounces of cheese). Soft cheese can provide one daily dairy serving without concern, as long as pasteurization is verified. However, soft cheese is calorie-dense and fat-rich; portion control based on overall nutrition and weight gain goals may be appropriate.
