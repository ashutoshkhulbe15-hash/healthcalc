# Can I Drink Soda During Pregnancy? Safety Guide

**Quick Answer:** ⚠️ **Caution** — Sugary sodas offer no nutritional value during pregnancy. Regular sodas contain caffeine (23-54mg per 12oz) that counts toward your 200mg daily limit. Diet sodas contain aspartame (generally safe) but avoid saccharin.

*Written by the ProHealthIt Editorial Team | Sources cited below*

---

## The Short Answer

The 3 PM craving hits—that cold, fizzy, sweet sensation of a just-opened soda bottle. It's intensely appealing, especially if you normally drink soda regularly. But soda carries multiple pregnancy concerns that stack against it. Regular sodas hit you with 39-40g of sugar per can (exceeding daily recommended added sugar), caffeine (23-54mg depending on type) that counts toward your 200mg limit, and zero nutritional value when pregnancy demands optimal nutrient density. Diet sodas skip sugar but contain aspartame (which is safe) or saccharin (which you should avoid). The broader issue transcends chemistry: soda displaces nutrient-dense beverages like milk, fortified alternatives, and water that pregnancy truly needs. ACOG doesn't forbid soda, but recommends treating it as an occasional indulgence rather than a regular beverage, especially given pregnancy's heightened nutritional demands.

---

## Why Soda Is Risky During Pregnancy

Pregnancy fundamentally changes nutritional requirements, increasing demands for calcium, iron, folate, vitamin D, and protein while simultaneously reducing total caloric allowance in the third trimester. Every beverage choice either advances or detracts from these requirements. Soda advances none of them while actively detracting in several ways.

Regular sodas deliver between 140-170 calories per 12-ounce serving consisting entirely of added sugars and caffeine. Consuming a single daily soda adds substantial calories without providing the micronutrients pregnancy demands. For pregnant people managing gestational diabetes risk—approximately 10% of pregnancies develop this condition—regular soda creates metabolic stress through rapid blood sugar elevation. The sugar also promotes dental erosion through acid exposure, which pregnancy hormones already exacerbate through increased gum sensitivity.

The caffeine content in sodas, while lower per serving than coffee, accumulates dangerously across the day. A Coke delivers approximately 34mg of caffeine per 12 ounces; Pepsi provides 38mg; Mountain Dew contains 54mg. Two sodas daily quickly approach the 200mg limit recommended by ACOG, eliminating room for caffeine from tea, chocolate, or other sources. Exceeding 200mg daily caffeine intake during pregnancy associates with increased miscarriage risk according to multiple epidemiological studies, making cumulative caffeine tracking essential.

Diet sodas replace sugar with synthetic sweeteners, addressing caloric and blood sugar concerns but not the fundamental issue: soda provides zero pregnancy nutrients. Aspartame, the sweetener in most diet sodas, breaks down into aspartic acid, phenylalanine, and methanol—components already present in foods and naturally produced during metabolism. Decades of research support aspartame safety during pregnancy at typical consumption levels. However, the beverage still occupies space in the diet that could be filled with milk supporting bone development, fortified plant milk providing vitamin D, or 100% fruit juice (in moderation) delivering vitamin C for iron absorption.

## How Sodas Affect Pregnancy Nutrition

Soda consumption directly undermines pregnancy nutrition in several mechanisms:

**Sugar and Metabolic Health:** Regular sodas contribute to excessive added sugar intake, associated with excessive gestational weight gain, gestational diabetes risk, and increased cesarean delivery rates in some studies. Guidelines suggest limiting added sugars to 6 teaspoons (25 grams) daily; a single 12-ounce soda exceeds this amount 1.5 times over. Repeated blood sugar spikes from soda consumption stress the pancreas and increase insulin resistance, particularly concerning in pregnancy when baseline insulin resistance naturally increases.

**Caffeine Accumulation:** Each soda consumed reduces the 200mg daily caffeine allowance available from other sources. A pregnant person drinking two 12-ounce sodas daily consumes 68-76mg of caffeine before any coffee, tea, or chocolate intake. This leaves minimal room for a morning coffee or afternoon tea without exceeding safe limits. For those with multiple dietary sources of caffeine, soda consumption often tips the balance into concerning territory.

**Tooth and Bone Health:** Soda's combination of acid and sugar creates particular dental risk during pregnancy. Pregnancy hormones increase gum inflammation and bleeding risk; soda's phosphoric and citric acids erode tooth enamel while sugars feed cavity-causing bacteria. Additionally, soda contains phosphoric acid, which in some studies associates with decreased calcium absorption—problematic when pregnancy requires 1,000mg daily calcium for fetal skeletal development.

**Nutrient Displacement:** The "displacement effect" represents soda's most insidious impact. Calories and time spent consuming soda replace consumption of milk (300mg calcium, 8 grams protein), fortified plant-based milk (8 grams protein, vitamin D, vitamin B12), or water enabling optimal hydration for increased blood volume. Pregnancy requires approximately 80-100 ounces of water daily; soda consumption often reduces water intake by displacement.

## Trimester-Specific Considerations

**First Trimester:** Early pregnancy nausea often drives soda cravings, as carbonation provides temporary relief for some pregnant people. While ginger ale or other ginger-based sodas might appeal, regular sodas should be avoided entirely. If carbonation genuinely helps nausea, sparkling water offers the same sensation without caffeine, sugar, and acid. This trimester demands maximum nutritional efficiency; soda's zero nutrient content becomes particularly problematic as fetal organ systems develop rapidly.

**Second Trimester:** As nausea typically resolves by week 13-14, soda cravings may intensify through other mechanisms: habit, desire for sweetness, or caffeine-seeking for energy. This trimester offers an opportunity to establish healthier beverage patterns supporting the remainder of pregnancy. Gestational diabetes screening occurs around week 24, making this an ideal time to eliminate regular soda and establish diabetes-preventive patterns. If soda consumption continues, strictly limit to occasional intake (once weekly at most) and factor any caffeine into daily totals.

**Third Trimester:** By final trimester, excessive weight gain becomes increasingly concerning for both maternal and fetal health outcomes. Soda consumption—whether regular (empty calories) or diet (problematic volume taking space from nutritious fluids)—should be minimized or eliminated entirely. Many pregnant people find carbonation increases reflux during this period, making soda doubly uncomfortable. This trimester demands optimal hydration from water and nutrient-dense beverages supporting labor preparation.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is diet soda safer than regular soda during pregnancy?**
Diet sodas eliminate the sugar and blood-sugar concerns of regular soda, addressing gestational diabetes risk. Aspartame, the sweetener in most diet sodas, has undergone extensive pregnancy safety research with reassuring results at typical consumption levels. However, diet soda remains problematic through caloric/nutrient displacement and caffeine content (many diet sodas actually contain equal or slightly higher caffeine than regular versions). From a pregnancy nutrition perspective, diet soda is marginally preferable but still represents a poor beverage choice compared to milk, water, or fortified alternatives.

**What about soda sweetened with stevia or erythritol?**
Stevia and erythritol represent newer sweetening options with growing safety data. Both are recognized as safe by the FDA during pregnancy. However, these beverages still contain caffeine in many formulations and provide zero nutritional benefit. The fundamental issue—nutrient displacement—persists regardless of sweetener type. If carbonation genuinely satisfies cravings, these options pose lower risks than traditional sodas, though sparkling water remains the optimal carbonated beverage for pregnancy.

**Can I drink soda if I'm not consuming other caffeine?**
Theoretically yes, but this approach requires careful attention to total caffeine accumulation. One 12-ounce soda delivers 23-54mg caffeine depending on type, leaving 146-177mg for the remainder of the day. Many pregnant people don't realize hidden caffeine sources—chocolate contains 5-10mg per ounce; green tea contains 25-50mg per cup; coffee ice cream contains variable amounts. Tracking becomes complicated. Rather than building a diet around soda's caffeine allowance, eliminate soda and reserve caffeine budget for beverages offering genuine pregnancy nutrition (tea for antioxidants, moderate coffee for energy support).

**Is clear soda (like Sprite or 7-Up) better than dark soda during pregnancy?**
Clear sodas avoid the phosphoric acid in colas (which may affect calcium absorption), but they contain citric acid affecting tooth enamel equally. Clear sodas still contain similar caffeine levels (29-34mg per 12oz) and identical sugar content. From a pregnancy standpoint, clear and dark sodas present equivalent risks. Both should be avoided in favor of sparkling water or other alternatives.

**What if I have cravings for soda specifically?**
Pregnancy cravings reflect complex neurochemical, hormonal, and sometimes nutritional needs. Carbonation cravings suggest: satisfaction of the sensation (sparkling water addresses this), habit formation (replacing with flavored sparkling water or herbal tea works), or caffeine-seeking (indicating potential fatigue worth addressing through sleep and nutrition). Address the underlying craving rather than indulging it through a non-nutritive beverage. Most soda cravings resolve within 2-3 weeks when replaced with satisfying alternatives like sparkling water with fresh fruit.
